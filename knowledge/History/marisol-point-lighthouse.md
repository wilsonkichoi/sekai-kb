The Marisol Point Lighthouse has not been needed for navigation since 1994, when the harbor commission decommissioned it in favor of a satellite beacon the size of a coffee tin. It is still lit every night. A rotation of volunteers climbs the forty-one steps to switch it on, and the reason they give is not maritime safety.[^1]

> **At a glance:** The tower is open for a free volunteer-led climb on the first Saturday of most months; the light itself is decorative now, run off a modern low-wattage lamp, and the original lens sits in a case at the base rather than in the lantern room.

## Built for the Fishing Fleet

The light was raised in 1889 on the low headland north of the harbor, after two winters in which fishing boats returning past [[lantern-cove-beach|Lantern Cove]] misread the shoreline in fog and grounded on the rocks. It was a working tool of the fishing town that the [[founding-of-marisol-cove|founding of Marisol Cove]] had built, and for a century its keepers were counted among the town's essential trades, alongside the boatwrights and the ice house.[^2]

The original lens was a fourth-order Fresnel, a tiered drum of hand-ground glass that threw a single fixed white beam roughly twelve miles out. Its keepers logged the weather every four hours, and those logs are the longest continuous local weather record the town holds.[^3]

## Made Obsolete, Kept Anyway

By the early 1990s the light was redundant. Boats navigated by GPS, and the harbor commission's 1994 report recommended shutting the tower and selling the headland. The recommendation went nowhere. By then the fishing fleet had mostly gone and the arts community had taken its place, and both groups, for different reasons, treated the tower as theirs: the last fishermen as the marker their fathers had steered by, the newer painters and boatwrights as the shape that appears in more local canvases than any other.

Rather than go dark, the light was handed to a volunteer preservation trust, which relit it as a civic symbol and opened the tower for public climbs. The lamp draws a fraction of the old power, and no boat depends on it.

## Visiting

- **The climb** — free, volunteer-led, first Saturday of most months; forty-one steps and a narrow final ladder, so it is not step-free.
- **Getting there** — a fifteen-minute walk north from the harbor along the headland path; the same path continues inland to the [[summit-ridge-trail|Summit Ridge Trail]].
- **The lens** — the original Fresnel lens is displayed at ground level and is worth the stop even when the tower is closed.

The trust keeps no gift shop and asks nothing at the door; a donation jar by the lens case pays for the lamp and the paint.
